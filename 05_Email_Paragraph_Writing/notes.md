# Email & Paragraph Writing — Concept Notes

---

## 1. Professional Email Structure

A formal business email follows a strict seven-part structural block. Adhering to this flow ensures clarity and professional decorum.

### The 7-Part Structural Block
1.  **Subject Line:** Clear, concise, and action-oriented. Never leave it blank or use generic words like "Urgent" or "Query."
    *   *Good:* "Request for Leave: June 28 - July 1 (Emp ID: 1234)"
    *   *Bad:* "Urgent leave"
2.  **Salutation:** Formal greeting using the recipient's name or title.
    *   *Formula:* "Dear Mr./Ms. [Last Name]," or "Dear [First Name]," (for colleagues). Use "Dear Hiring Manager," or "Dear Sir/Madam," if the recipient is unknown.
3.  **Purpose Statement:** The first sentence of the body should state the reason for writing immediately.
    *   *Examples:* "I am writing to request..." or "I am writing to follow up on..."
4.  **Details/Body:** Outline the necessary context, bulleted points, or explanations in 2-3 sentences.
5.  **Call to Action (Request):** Explicitly state what you need the recipient to do next.
    *   *Examples:* "Could you please confirm your availability by tomorrow?" or "I look forward to your approval."
6.  **Thank You / Closing:** A polite wrap-up sentence.
    *   *Examples:* "Thank you for your time and consideration." or "I appreciate your prompt assistance."
7.  **Regards & Sign-off:** Formal closing followed by your full name and details.
    *   *Examples:* "Sincerely," or "Best regards," followed by your name and designation on a new line.

## 2. Paragraph Structure

A well-written academic or professional paragraph centers on a single topic and maintains clear cohesive links.

### The 4-Part Paragraph Block
1.  **Introduction (Topic Sentence):** The opening sentence that states the main idea of the paragraph.
2.  **Explanation (Elaboration):** 2-3 sentences explaining the topic sentence in more detail, presenting supporting details or logic.
3.  **Concrete Example:** A real-world example, case study, or statistic that illustrates the explanation.
4.  **Conclusion:** A final sentence that wraps up the paragraph's thought and links it back to the main topic.

### Cohesive Linkers (Transition Words)
To ensure smooth transitions between sentences:
*   *For adding ideas:* "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover."
*   *For contrasting ideas:* "However," "On the contrary," "In contrast."
*   *For demonstrating cause-effect:* "Consequently," "Therefore," "As a result."
*   *For introducing examples:* "For instance," "To illustrate," "Specifically."
