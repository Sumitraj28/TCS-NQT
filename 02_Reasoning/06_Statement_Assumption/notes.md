# Statement & Assumption — Complete Notes

---

## 1. Defining Statement and Assumption

Critical reasoning questions on Statements and Assumptions test your ability to identify unstated premises.

*   **Statement:** A declaration or sentence made by an author.
*   **Assumption:** Something taken for granted, assumed, or accepted as true *before* making the statement. It is the unstated link or foundation that supports the statement.

### The "Before" vs. "After" Rule (Crucial Distinction)
The main source of confusion is distinguishing between an Assumption and a Conclusion:
*   An **Assumption** is the premise that goes **before** the statement. It must be true for the statement to make sense.
*   A **Conclusion** is the logical deduction that follows **after** the statement. It is a direct consequence of the statement.

---

## 2. Key Differences: Assumption vs. Conclusion

| Feature | Assumption | Conclusion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chronology** | Exists **before** the statement is made. | Derived **after** the statement is read. |
| **Visibility** | **Unstated**; hidden between the lines. | Can be directly **deduced** or paraphrased from the text. |
| **Relation** | Foundation/Premise. | Result/Consequence. |
| **Test Method** | **Negation Test:** If you negate the assumption and the statement falls apart, the assumption is valid. | **Logical Derivation Test:** If the statement is true, this must definitely be true. |

### The Negation Test (The Ultimate Tool for Assumptions)
To check if an assumption is valid:
1.  Negate the assumption (make it opposite).
2.  Read the statement with the negated assumption.
3.  If the statement now sounds illogical or useless, then the assumption is **valid**.
4.  *Example:*
    *   *Statement:* "Please use the elevator to go to the 10th floor."
    *   *Assumption:* "The elevator is operational."
    *   *Negated Assumption:* "The elevator is NOT operational."
    *   *Check:* If the elevator is not working, telling people to use it is useless. The statement falls apart. Thus, the assumption is **valid**.

---

## 3. Rules to Identify Valid Assumptions

1.  **Implicit Message:** An assumption is always implicit. An assumption that restates the statement exactly is invalid.
2.  **No Generalizations:** Assumptions containing words like "all," "always," "every," or "never" are usually invalid (too extreme), whereas words like "some," "many," "may," or "can" are usually valid.
3.  **Advertisements/Notices:** For any advertisement or notice, it is always assumed that:
    *   People read notices/advertisements.
    *   People will respond to or follow the instructions.
4.  **Government/Authority Decisions:** Any directive or policy by the government/authority is assumed to be implementable and beneficial.
