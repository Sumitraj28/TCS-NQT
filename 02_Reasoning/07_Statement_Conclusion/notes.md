# Statement & Conclusion — Complete Notes

---

## 1. Defining Statement and Conclusion

Statement and Conclusion questions evaluate your ability to make logical deductions.

*   **Statement:** A factual declaration or set of sentences that you must accept as $100\%$ true, without bringing in any outside general knowledge.
*   **Conclusion:** A logical deduction or inference that is derived directly **from** the statement. It must be true if the statement is true.

### The "Before" vs. "After" Rule (Crucial Distinction)
Understanding the difference between an Assumption and a Conclusion is critical:
*   An **Assumption** is the unstated premise that goes **before** the statement. It is what the author took for granted when speaking.
*   A **Conclusion** is the logical deduction that follows **after** the statement. It is a fact that is directly supported by the statement.

---

## 2. Key Differences: Conclusion vs. Assumption

| Feature | Conclusion | Assumption |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chronology** | Derived **after** reading the statement. | Exists **before** the statement is made. |
| **Visibility** | Directly **deduced** from the text. | **Unstated**; hidden between the lines. |
| **Relation** | Result/Consequence. | Foundation/Premise. |
| **Test Method** | **Logical Derivation Test:** If the statement is true, this must definitely be true. | **Negation Test:** If you negate it and the statement falls apart, it is valid. |

### The Logical Derivation Test
To check if a conclusion is valid:
1.  Assume the statement is the absolute truth.
2.  Check if the conclusion is a direct mathematical or logical subset of the statement.
3.  If the conclusion requires any external assumption or "mind-reading" not supported by the statement, it is **invalid**.

---

## 3. Rules to Identify Valid Conclusions

1.  **Strict Adherence to Statement:** Do not assume anything outside the scope of the statement. Avoid using general knowledge.
2.  **No Generalizations:** Conclusions containing extreme words like "all," "always," "every," "no," or "never" are typically invalid, while words like "some," "many," "often," or "may" are generally valid.
3.  **Advice/Cure Connection:** If a statement suggests a course of action or cure, the conclusion that the problem exists is valid.
4.  **No Assumed Cause-Effect:** Do not assume a cause-effect relationship unless it is explicitly mentioned in the statement.
