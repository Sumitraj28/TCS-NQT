# Profit & Loss Common Mistakes

Avoid these 15 common mistakes to keep from falling into exam traps during the NQT.

---

## 1. Calculating Profit/Loss Percentage on SP
*   **Mistake**: Calculating profit or loss as a percentage of the Selling Price rather than the Cost Price.
*   **Why it happens**: Lack of conceptual grounding in percentage reference bases.
*   **Correct Approach**: Always divide by Cost Price (CP) in the denominator: $\frac{\text{Profit}}{CP} \times 100$.
*   **Example**: Bought for ₹80, sold for ₹100.
    *   *Wrong*: $\frac{20}{100} \times 100 = 20\%$ profit.
    *   *Right*: $\frac{20}{80} \times 100 = 25\%$ profit.

---

## 2. Linear Addition of Successive Discounts
*   **Mistake**: Adding successive discounts of $20\%$ and $10\%$ to get $30\%$.
*   **Why it happens**: Treating compounding discounts as simple linear subtractions.
*   **Correct Approach**: Apply the successive discount formula: $d_1 + d_2 - \frac{d_1 d_2}{100}\%$.
*   **Example**: Successive discounts of $20\%$ and $10\%$.
    *   *Wrong*: $20\% + 10\% = 30\%$ discount.
    *   *Right*: $20 + 10 - \frac{200}{100} = 28\%$ discount.

---

## 3. Dishonest Dealer Denominator Swap
*   **Mistake**: Dividing by the true weight (e.g. 1000g) instead of the delivered weight (e.g. 900g).
*   **Why it happens**: Assuming the nominal benchmark weight is the investment base.
*   **Correct Approach**: The dealer's investment is the actual goods he delivers. Use delivered weight as the base.
*   **Example**: Uses 900g instead of 1kg.
    *   *Wrong*: $\frac{100}{1000} \times 100 = 10\%$ profit.
    *   *Right*: $\frac{100}{900} \times 100 = 11.11\%$ profit.

---

## 4. Calculating Discounts on Cost Price
*   **Mistake**: Subtracting a discount directly from the Cost Price.
*   **Why it happens**: Confusing the cost base with the retail marked base.
*   **Correct Approach**: Discounts must be applied to the Marked Price (MP).
*   **Example**: CP = ₹100, MP = ₹120. Discount = $10\%$.
    *   *Wrong*: $SP = 100 - 10 = ₹90$.
    *   *Right*: $SP = 120 - 12 = ₹108$.

---

## 5. Adding Markup and Discount Percentages Linearly
*   **Mistake**: Assuming a $20\%$ markup followed by a $20\%$ discount results in no profit or loss.
*   **Why it happens**: Forgetting that the markup base (CP) is smaller than the discount base (MP).
*   **Correct Approach**: Apply successive changes: $+20\%$ and $-20\% \implies -4\%$ (net loss).
*   **Example**: CP = ₹100, Markup = $20\%$, Discount = $20\%$.
    *   *Wrong*: $SP = ₹100$ ($0\%$ profit).
    *   *Right*: $SP = 100 \times 1.20 \times 0.80 = ₹96$ ($4\%$ loss).

---

## 6. The Equal SP Symmetry Trap
*   **Mistake**: Assuming that selling two items at the same price, one at $10\%$ profit and the other at $10\%$ loss, results in break-even.
*   **Why it happens**: Forgetting that the cost prices are different, making the absolute loss larger than the absolute gain.
*   **Correct Approach**: Use the net loss shortcut: $(\frac{x}{10})^2\%$.
*   **Example**: Two items sold for ₹9,900 each, $+10\%$ and $-10\%$.
    *   *Wrong*: No profit, no loss (break-even).
    *   *Right*: $1\%$ loss.

---

## 7. Ratio Reversal in "CP of X = SP of Y"
*   **Mistake**: Writing the ratio $\frac{SP}{CP}$ as $\frac{Y}{X}$ instead of $\frac{X}{Y}$.
*   **Why it happens**: Rushing to transpose terms across the equals sign.
*   **Correct Approach**: Cross-multiply carefully: $X \times CP = Y \times SP \implies \frac{SP}{CP} = \frac{X}{Y}$.
*   **Example**: CP of 10 articles = SP of 8 articles.
    *   *Wrong*: Ratio $= 8/10 \implies 20\%$ loss.
    *   *Right*: Ratio $= 10/8 = 5/4 \implies 25\%$ profit.

---

## 8. Ignoring Overhead Charges in CP
*   **Mistake**: Excluding transport or repair costs from the Cost Price base when calculating profit.
*   **Why it happens**: Treating purchase price as the sole cost parameter.
*   **Correct Approach**: Total CP = Purchase Price + Overheads.
*   **Example**: Bought a machine for ₹10,000, spent ₹2,000 on transport, sold for ₹15,000.
    *   *Wrong*: Profit $= \frac{5000}{10000} \times 100 = 50\%$.
    *   *Right*: Total CP = 12,000. Profit $= \frac{3000}{12000} \times 100 = 25\%$.

---

## 9. Buy X, Get Y Free Base Error
*   **Mistake**: Dividing the free items by the paid items to find the discount percentage.
*   **Why it happens**: Using the paid quantity as the denominator.
*   **Correct Approach**: Divide by the total items received: $\frac{Y}{X+Y}$.
*   **Example**: Buy 4, get 1 free.
    *   *Wrong*: $\frac{1}{4} \times 100 = 25\%$ discount.
    *   *Right*: $\frac{1}{5} \times 100 = 20\%$ discount.

---

## 10. Reversing CP and SP Multipliers
*   **Mistake**: Calculating CP from SP by multiplying by the profit factor (e.g. $CP = SP \times 1.20$ for a $20\%$ profit).
*   **Why it happens**: Reversing the mathematical scaling path.
*   **Correct Approach**: Divide SP by the profit factor: $CP = \frac{SP}{1.20}$.
*   **Example**: SP = ₹120, Profit = $20\%$.
    *   *Wrong*: $CP = 120 \times 0.8 = ₹96$ or $CP = 120 \times 1.2 = ₹144$.
    *   *Right*: $CP = \frac{120}{1.20} = ₹100$.

---

## 11. Dishonest Dealer Double-Sided Weight Mistake
*   **Mistake**: Writing the buying cheat ratio incorrectly as $\frac{900}{1000}$ instead of $\frac{1100}{1000}$.
*   **Why it happens**: Confusing cheating when buying (getting more) with cheating when selling (giving less).
*   **Correct Approach**: Buying cheat of $10\%$ means getting $1100$g for the price of $1000$g.
*   **Example**: Cheats by $10\%$ buying, sells at CP.
    *   *Wrong*: Profit $= 10\%$.
    *   *Right*: Profit $= 10\%$ (weight factor $= 1.10$).

---

## 12. Incorrect Mixture Cost Price Scaling
*   **Mistake**: Using the selling price of a mixture directly in alligation calculations when a profit margin is included.
*   **Why it happens**: Mixing SP and CP bases in the same alligation diagram.
*   **Correct Approach**: Convert the mixture SP to mixture CP first, then apply alligation.
*   **Example**: Blend wheat at ₹30/kg and ₹40/kg, sell mixture at ₹38.5/kg to make $10\%$ profit.
    *   *Wrong*: Use 38.5 directly as the mean value.
    *   *Right*: Mean CP $= \frac{38.5}{1.10} = ₹35/\text{kg}$. Use 35 as the mean value.

---

## 13. Confusing Marked Price with Selling Price
*   **Mistake**: Assuming the Marked Price is the price at which the transaction occurs.
*   **Why it happens**: Forgetting that discounts alter the final transaction value.
*   **Correct Approach**: Selling Price = Marked Price - Discount.
*   **Example**: Marked at ₹500, discount of $10\%$.
    *   *Wrong*: SP = ₹500.
    *   *Right*: SP = ₹450.

---

## 14. Adding Profit margins across steps linearly
*   **Mistake**: If A sells to B at $10\%$ profit and B sells to C at $20\%$ profit, A's net profit if he sold directly is assumed to be $30\%$.
*   **Why it happens**: Ignoring compounding scaling.
*   **Correct Approach**: Apply successive changes: $10 + 20 + \frac{200}{100} = 32\%$ net profit.

---

## 15. Typing Unit Labels into the NQT FIB Blank
*   **Mistake**: Typing `20%` or `₹150` or `120 ways` into the NQT numerical input blank.
*   **Why it happens**: Habit of writing units with answers.
*   **Correct Approach**: Type numbers only (e.g. `20`, `150`, `120`). TCS grading software marks inputs containing letters or symbols as incorrect.
