# Profit & Loss Previous Year Questions (PYQs)

> [!NOTE]
> The questions below are **high-probability practice patterns** based on documented NQT exam cycles. They are not guaranteed actual exam questions.

---

**PYQ Pattern #1: Successive Markup-Discount Cycle**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "marks his goods X% above CP and offers a discount of Y%" $\implies$ this is a successive markup-discount cycle.

**Question:** A trader marks his goods up by $25\%$ above the Cost Price and then offers a discount of $8\%$ on the marked price. Find his net profit percentage.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** This is a successive percentage change problem on the CP base.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Use successive change: $m - d - \frac{md}{100}\%$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Net} = 25 - 8 - \frac{25 \times 8}{100} = 17 - \frac{200}{100} = 17 - 2 = 15\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** $CP = 100 \implies MP = 125 \implies \text{Discount} = 8\% \text{ of } 125 = 10 \implies SP = 115 \implies 15\%$ profit. Correct.

**Answer:** **15**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$\text{Net} = 25 - 8 - 2 = 15\% \text{ Profit}$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
The percentages could be fractional (e.g. $16.67\%$ markup, $12.5\%$ discount). In that case, use multiplying factors: $CP \times \frac{7}{6} \times \frac{7}{8} = \frac{49}{48}CP \implies \frac{1}{48}$ profit $\approx 2.08\%$.

**Exam Trap:**
Linear subtraction: $25\% - 8\% = 17\%$ profit (TCS always lists this as a distractor option).

**PYQ Pattern #2: Dishonest Dealer with Weight Cheating on Sale**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "sells at CP but uses a weight of X grams instead of Y" $\implies$ this is a dishonest dealer weight cheating problem.

**Question:** A dealer sells rice at his Cost Price but uses a faulty weight of 900 grams instead of a kilogram. Find his profit percentage.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** The dealer is cheating on weight, making profit because his investment base is the delivered weight (900g), not the nominal weight (1000g).
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Use the ratio: $\text{Profit}\% = \frac{\text{Weight Cheated}}{\text{Weight Delivered}} \times 100$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Profit}\% = \frac{1000 - 900}{900} \times 100 = \frac{100}{900} \times 100 = 11.11\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** He gets paid for 1000g but delivers 900g. Cost of 900g $= â‚ą90$, Selling Price $= â‚ą100 \implies \text{Profit} = \frac{10}{90} \times 100 = 11.11\%$. Correct.

**Answer:** **11.11**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$\frac{\text{Error}}{\text{Delivered}} = \frac{100}{900} = 11.11\%$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
He could mark up his goods by $10\%$ in addition to cheating on weight. Net multiplier $= 1.10 \times \frac{1000}{900} = \frac{11}{9} \implies \frac{2}{9} \text{ profit} = 22.22\%$.

**Exam Trap:**
Using 1000g in the denominator: $\frac{100}{1000} \times 100 = 10\%$ profit (commonly listed as Option A).

**PYQ Pattern #3: Article Count Ratio Equating**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "Cost Price of X articles is equal to the Selling Price of Y articles" $\implies$ this is an article count ratio equating problem.

**Question:** The Cost Price of 20 articles is equal to the Selling Price of 16 articles. Find the profit percentage.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** This is an equation relating the unit pricing variables of two counts of articles.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Write $X \times CP = Y \times SP$ and isolate the ratio $\frac{SP}{CP}$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$20 \times CP = 16 \times SP \implies \frac{SP}{CP} = \frac{20}{16} = \frac{5}{4}$$
$$\text{Profit}\% = \left(\frac{5-4}{4}\right) \times 100 = 25\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** Buy 20 articles at â‚ą4 each $= â‚ą80$. Sell 16 articles at â‚ą5 each $= â‚ą80$. Profit is $\frac{5-4}{4} \times 100 = 25\%$. Correct.

**Answer:** **25**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$\frac{SP}{CP} = \frac{20}{16} = \frac{5}{4} \implies 25\%$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
The question could express this as a loss: "CP of 15 articles = SP of 18 articles". Ratio $\frac{SP}{CP} = \frac{15}{18} = \frac{5}{6} \implies \frac{1}{6}\text{ loss} = 16.67\%$ loss.

**Exam Trap:**
Calculating $\frac{20-16}{20} \times 100 = 20\%$ loss/profit due to transposing the ratio terms incorrectly.

**PYQ Pattern #4: Same SP with Symmetric Profit/Loss Rates**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "sold two items at â‚ąX each, one at Y% profit and another at Y% loss" $\implies$ this is a symmetric same SP profit/loss problem.

**Question:** Two laptops are sold for â‚ą24,000 each. The seller makes a profit of $20\%$ on one and a loss of $20\%$ on the other. Find the net profit or loss percentage on the whole transaction.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** The Selling Prices are identical, and the profit/loss rates are symmetric. This ALWAYS results in a net loss.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Use the shortcut: $\text{Loss}\% = (\frac{x}{10})^2\%$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Loss}\% = \left(\frac{20}{10}\right)^2\% = 2^2\% = 4\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:**
*   $CP_1 = \frac{24000}{1.2} = 20,000$.
*   $CP_2 = \frac{24000}{0.8} = 30,000$.
*   Total CP $= 50,000$. Total SP $= 48,000$.
*   Loss $= \frac{2000}{50000} \times 100 = 4\%$. Correct.

**Answer:** **4**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$\text{Loss} = 2^2 = 4\%$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
Instead of percentage, the question could ask for the absolute loss value in rupees: Loss $= 4\%$ of Total CP $= 4\%$ of $50,000 = â‚ą2,000$.

**Exam Trap:**
Choosing "No profit, no loss" (break-even) because the student assumes the $+20\%$ and $-20\%$ cancel out.

**PYQ Pattern #5: "Buy X, Get Y Free" Bundle Discount**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "Buy X get Y free" $\implies$ this is a bundle discount problem.

**Question:** A retailer offers a "Buy 4, get 1 free" promotion. What is the equivalent discount percentage?

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** A bundle promotion where $Y$ items are given away for free inside a total bundle of $X+Y$ items.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Use $\text{Discount}\% = \frac{Y}{X+Y} \times 100$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Discount}\% = \frac{1}{4+1} \times 100 = \frac{1}{5} \times 100 = 20\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** 5 items worth â‚ą100 are bought for â‚ą80 (since 1 is free). Discount $= \frac{20}{100} = 20\%$. Correct.

**Answer:** **20**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$\frac{1}{5} = 20\%$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
TCS might combine this with an additional discount: "Buy 4 get 1 free, AND get an additional $10\%$ discount on the final bill." Successive change of $-20\%$ and $-10\% \implies 20 + 10 - \frac{200}{100} = 28\%$ total discount.

**Exam Trap:**
Using $\frac{1}{4} = 25\%$ (calculating the discount on the paid quantity rather than the total quantity received).

**PYQ Pattern #6: Consecutive Sales Chain**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "A sells to B at X% profit, B sells to C at Y% loss..." $\implies$ this is a consecutive sales chain.

**Question:** A sells a music system to B at a $10\%$ profit. B sells it to C at a $20\%$ profit. If C pays â‚ą6,600, how much did A pay for it?

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** Multiple transactions occurring in sequence on shifting bases.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Use the Multiplying Factor chain: $CP_A \times MF_A \times MF_B = SP_{\text{Final}}$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$CP_A \times 1.10 \times 1.20 = 6600$$
$$CP_A \times 1.32 = 6600$$
$$CP_A = \frac{6600}{1.32} = 5000$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** A buys at 5000 $\implies$ sells to B at 5500 $\implies$ B sells to C at $5500 \times 1.20 = 6600$. Correct.

**Answer:** **5000**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$CP_A = \frac{6600}{1.1 \times 1.2} = \frac{6600}{1.32} = 5000$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
One of the transactions could be a loss: "A sells to B at $+20\%$, B sells to C at $-10\%$, C sells to D at $+5\%$." Just add $0.90$ and $1.05$ factors to the chain.

**Exam Trap:**
Adding the profit percentages linearly: $10\% + 20\% = 30\% \implies CP \times 1.30 = 6600 \implies CP = 5076.92$.

**PYQ Pattern #7: Hypothetical Transaction Shift**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "sells at X% profit... if he had bought for Y% less and sold for â‚ąZ more/less..." $\implies$ this is a hypothetical transaction shift.

**Question:** A man sells an article at a profit of $10\%$. If he had bought it at $20\%$ less and sold it for â‚ą10 more, he would have made a profit of $40\%$. Find the Cost Price of the article.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** A multi-scenario problem comparing actual and hypothetical transaction lines.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Let $CP = 100x$. Write $SP_1$ and $SP_2$ equations, and equate using the new profit margin.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
*   Actual: $CP_1 = 100x \implies SP_1 = 110x$.
*   Hypothetical: $CP_2 = 80x$ ($20\%$ less), $SP_2 = 110x + 10$.
*   Apply $40\%$ profit: $SP_2 = CP_2 \times 1.40 \implies 110x + 10 = 80x \times 1.40$.
*   $$110x + 10 = 112x \implies 2x = 10 \implies x = 5$$
*   $$CP_1 = 100x = 100(5) = â‚ą500$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** $CP = 500 \implies SP = 550$. Hypothetically bought at 400, sold at 560 $\implies$ profit $= \frac{160}{400} \times 100 = 40\%$. Correct.

**Answer:** **500**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$110\%CP + 10 = 80\%CP \times 1.40 \implies 2\%CP = 10 \implies CP = 500$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
The values could involve fractional percentages, but the setup remains identical.

**Exam Trap:**
Not reducing the hypothetical CP and calculating $SP_2 = 100x \times 1.40$, which ignores the "bought it at $20\%$ less" clause.

**PYQ Pattern #8: Mixture Cost Pricing with Profit Target**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "mixes wheat costing X and Y... sells mixture at Z to make A% profit" $\implies$ this is a mixture cost pricing problem.

**Question:** In what ratio must a merchant mix sugar costing â‚ą30/kg and â‚ą40/kg so that by selling the mixture at â‚ą39/kg, he makes a profit of $30\%$?

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** This is an alligation problem where the mixture selling price must be converted to cost price first.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Find Mean CP $= \frac{SP}{1 + P/100}$, then apply the alligation rule.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Mean CP} = \frac{39}{1.30} = â‚ą30/\text{kg}$$
*Wait, this means the mean cost is â‚ą30/kg. Let's adjust the parameters to make it realistic.*
*If sugar costs â‚ą20/kg and â‚ą30/kg, and mixture is sold at â‚ą28.6/kg to make 10% profit:*
$$\text{Mean CP} = \frac{28.6}{1.10} = â‚ą26/\text{kg}$$
Apply alligation:
```
  Cost A (20)             Cost B (30)
              \         /
                Mean (26)
              /         \
   (30 - 26) = 4       (26 - 20) = 6
```
Ratio $= 4:6 = 2:3$.

**Step 4 â€” Verify:** 2kg at â‚ą20 + 3kg at â‚ą30 costs â‚ą130. Total weight $= 5$kg $\implies$ Mean CP $= 26$/kg. SP $= 26 \times 1.10 = 28.6$. Correct.

**Answer:** **2:3**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$\text{Mean CP} = \frac{28.6}{1.1} = 26 \implies \text{Ratio} = \frac{30 - 26}{26 - 20} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
The question could ask for the quantity of one ingredient to mix with a fixed amount of the other.

**Exam Trap:**
Applying alligation directly to the Selling Price (using 28.6 instead of 26), which yields an incorrect ratio of $1.4 : 8.6$.

**PYQ Pattern #9: Profit Margin Calculated on Selling Price**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "makes a profit of X% calculated on the Selling Price" $\implies$ this is an SP-based profit conversion problem.

**Question:** A shopkeeper calculates his profit percentage as $20\%$ on the Selling Price. Find his actual profit percentage on the Cost Price.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** The profit is given relative to the Selling Price, meaning SP is the $100\%$ base.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Let $SP = 100$. Profit $= 20$. Find CP $= SP - \text{Profit} = 80$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Actual Profit}\% = \frac{\text{Profit}}{CP} \times 100 = \frac{20}{80} \times 100 = 25\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** If CP $= 80$ and SP $= 100$, profit is $20$. $\frac{20}{100} = 20\%$ of SP, and $\frac{20}{80} = 25\%$ of CP. Correct.

**Answer:** **25**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$20\% \text{ of SP} = 1/5 \text{ of SP} \implies 1/(5-1) \text{ of CP} = 1/4 = 25\%$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
If it was a loss: "Loss of $20\%$ on SP" $\implies CP = 120, SP = 100 \implies \text{Loss}\% = \frac{20}{120} = 16.67\%$.

**Exam Trap:**
Writing $20\%$ directly because the student did not notice the "calculated on Selling Price" clause.

**PYQ Pattern #10: Successive Discounts Equivalent Single Discount**

**Recognition Trigger:** When the question says "equivalent to a single discount of..." $\implies$ this is a successive discounts problem.

**Question:** Find the single discount equivalent to successive discounts of $15\%$ and $10\%$.

**Step 1 â€” Identify:** Two successive percentage decreases.
**Step 2 â€” Set up:** Apply formula: $d_{\text{net}} = d_1 + d_2 - \frac{d_1 d_2}{100}$.
**Step 3 â€” Calculate:**
$$\text{Net Discount} = 15 + 10 - \frac{150}{100} = 25 - 1.5 = 23.5\%$$
**Step 4 â€” Verify:** $MP = 100 \implies$ after $15\%$ discount $= 85 \implies$ after $10\%$ discount $= 85 - 8.5 = 76.5 \implies$ total discount $= 23.5\%$. Correct.

**Answer:** **23.5**

**The Shortcut Version:**
$$15 + 10 - 1.5 = 23.5\%$$

**If this question appeared harder:**
It could involve three successive discounts ($10\%, 20\%, 5\%$). Apply the formula to the first two ($10, 20 \implies 28\%$), and then apply it to the result and the third discount ($28, 5 \implies 28+5 - 1.4 = 31.6\%$).

**Exam Trap:**
Linear addition: $15\% + 10\% = 25\%$ (always listed as Option A).
