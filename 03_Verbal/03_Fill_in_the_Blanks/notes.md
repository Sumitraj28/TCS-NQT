# Fill in the Blanks — Complete Notes

---

## 1. Single-Blank Questions

Single-blank questions test vocabulary breadth and contextual grammar.

### Core Strategy
1.  **Read without options:** Read the sentence and try to predict what kind of word (positive/negative, part of speech) belongs in the blank.
2.  **Locate Clue Words:** Look for transition words that reveal the logical direction of the sentence.
    *   *Support Transitions:* "and," "moreover," "therefore," "because" $\rightarrow$ the blank will match the tone of the rest of the sentence.
    *   *Contrast Transitions:* "but," "yet," "although," "despite" $\rightarrow$ the blank will have a tone opposite to a key word in the sentence.
3.  **Grammar Check:** Pay attention to words directly before and after the blank.
    *   *Example:* An empty blank preceded by the article **"an"** immediately eliminates choices starting with consonant sounds.

---

## 2. Double-Blank Questions

Double-blank questions require matching two words that satisfy the sentence simultaneously.

### Core Strategy
1.  **Solve one blank at a time:** Do not try to evaluate both blanks at once. Focus on the blank that has clearer contextual clues (usually the second blank).
2.  **Eliminate options in columns:** If a word does not fit the first blank, eliminate the entire option immediately. Do not spend time checking if the second word fits.
3.  **Check for Relationship:** Often, the two blanks are related by a transition word (e.g., "Although the plan was [blank], the execution was [blank]").
    *   Since "although" signals contrast, look for an option where word 1 and word 2 are antonyms or have opposite tones (e.g., *brilliant* / *flawed*).

---

## 3. Flagged Example (Double-Blank)
*   **Question:** Although the CEO's speech was ________, it failed to ________ the disgruntled shareholders.
    *   *Options:*
        *   A. tedious, annoy
        *   B. eloquent, appease
        *   C. verbose, irritate
        *   D. brief, concern
    *   *Analysis:*
        1.  "Although" indicates contrast. The first blank describes the speech positively (or neutrally) if the second blank represents a failure of a positive action.
        2.  The phrase "failed to [blank] the disgruntled shareholders" implies we wanted to satisfy or calm them down. So blank 2 should mean "pacify" or "please".
        3.  Let's look at the second words in the options: *annoy* (negative), *appease* (positive - means pacify), *irritate* (negative), *concern* (neutral).
        4.  *Appease* fits blank 2 perfectly. Let's check word 1: *eloquent* (positive - means fluent speaker).
        5.  "Although the speech was eloquent, it failed to appease..." makes perfect logical sense.
    *   *Answer:* **B**
